Background

For this project, I worked in a team of five to build a bio-inspired toy for fourth graders. Through the course of the project, I went through the design process, starting from learning about the user group to iterating through mechanical prototypes. I learned a lot about project scheduling and working on a large team.

Design Process

We began the design process for this project by coming up with different types of motion we could have our toy do and different types of games fourth graders could play. We put all these ideas on sticky notes and put them on posters. We then attempted to mix and match different games with different motion styles. This gave us five concepts, which we then elaborated on. Eventually, we settled on the jungle river game, with a swinging motion.

Figure 1. Our results from our three ideation sessions.

Bio-Inspired Design

One of the main goals of this project was to design our toy after the motion of a real animal. Our toy was based on the motion of a gibbon. In order to design a mechanical system, we needed to completely understand and visualize the swinging motion of the gibbon, and condense its complex motion into something we could build into a toy. We managed to simplify the swinging motion into two specific movements, which helped us get a start on mechanical design.

Figure 2. One of our team members acting out the motion of our toy.

Mechanical Design

Mechanically, there were many small subsystems that needed to work together for our gibbon to swing across monkey bars. I was given the responsibility of designing the legs, which were an integral part of the game. The legs I ended up designing were laser cut out of delrin. The lower leg is always vertical, and the upper legs are attached to a servo, which allows the user to pick up items from the ground with a magnet, as the gibbon swings along the monkey bars.
